# Caps Kids — Global Childhood Nutrition Challenges Across the AWANA Footprint

**A country-by-country case for childhood food literacy as ministry**
*Prepared for the Captain Culinary Kids / AWANA International partnership track — April 2026*

---

## Framing

AWANA reaches roughly 11 million children every week across more than 140 countries, and its public-school work in Africa alone is built around an open door to potentially 31 million children on the continent. Across Africa, Awana brings biblical curriculum and educator training to public schools, and as of 2025, 4,487,482 boys and girls in 10,764 schools are part of that work. The Africa Child & School Initiative began in Zimbabwe and now operates across roughly 16 African countries; Awana also works in 27 European countries plus North and Central Asia. Each of those countries has children. Each of those countries has a different story about what's broken in the way those children eat — and a different opening for Caps Kids to do real good.

The problems sort, broadly, into three families. In sub-Saharan Africa, the dominant pattern is **chronic undernutrition** — stunting, wasting, and micronutrient deficiency caused by poverty, monotonous diets, and disrupted supply. In Western and Southern Europe, the dominant pattern is **childhood obesity driven by ultra-processed foods**, falling hardest on the poorest households. In post-Soviet Central Asia and Eastern Europe, the pattern is the **triple burden** — undernutrition, overweight, and micronutrient gaps coexisting in the same child population, often in the same family. Caps Kids — Cap the robot, the plate library in the Cook's Illustrated visual register, the on-device Gemma model that runs without connectivity, the 5th-grade-and-up curriculum — is built to address all three. What follows lays out, country by country, what's actually wrong and what Caps Kids can specifically do about it.

## AFRICA — The Africa Child & School Initiative Footprint

### Zimbabwe

Zimbabwe is the anchor of AWANA's Africa Child & School Initiative, and it is also a country in the middle of a slow-motion nutrition collapse. Stunting among Zimbabwean children under five rose from 23.5% to 27%, and the share of overweight under-fives climbed from 2.5% to 4%, while a 2024 ZimLAC assessment placed half the country's population — 7.6 million people — in food insecurity, with child wasting rising from 4.1% to 4.9% in a single year.

Zimbabwe is the country where Caps Kids has the most natural soil. The Awana school program already has the relational infrastructure — Mt Darwin and Mashonaland classrooms where teachers describe behavior change, attendance gains, and reading breakthroughs from the existing curriculum. Adding Caps Kids to that pipe means children who are already showing up for Awana also gain a structured, age-banded path into food literacy: how to recognize an ear of maize as the start of nshima, how to build a plate from sadza, leafy greens, and beans, how to think about a meal as something they can construct rather than something that does or doesn't appear. The on-device Gemma 4 E4B build matters here in a way it does not in Knoxville: rural Mashonaland is not a connected place, and a cooking tutor that runs on a $60 Android phone with no signal is the version that actually works in the village. The mirepoix plate becomes the sadza-and-greens plate. The robot teaches what the grandmother already knows.

### Kenya

Kenya is one of the clearest cases of the "triple burden" anywhere on the continent: undernutrition, micronutrient deficiency, and overnutrition all rising together. More than a quarter of Kenyan children under five are stunted, with stunting reaching 46% in Kitui and West Pokot counties, while at the same time UNICEF projects Kenya could have one million obese children aged 5-19 by 2030, driven by ultra-processed food marketing and a shift away from traditional diets.

Caps Kids fits Kenya precisely because Kenya's challenge is no longer a single-direction problem. A program that only fights hunger misses the urban Nairobi child being marketed sugary drinks; a program that only fights obesity misses the child in West Pokot getting a maize-and-beans school meal that lacks vegetables and protein diversity. The plate library, with its emphasis on whole-food assembly across multiple cuisines and its built-in vegetable-forward logic, teaches a Kenyan kid the same underlying skill regardless of which side of the burden they're on: read the plate, build the plate, understand what's missing. The 5th-grade age floor is well-suited here too — Kenya's adolescent obesity surge happens between roughly 10 and 19, exactly the window Caps Kids enters.

### Tanzania

Tanzania has cut child stunting roughly in half over three decades, but the problem remains stubborn and uneven, and a quiet adult obesity wave is rising behind it. Stunting in Tanzania fell from 50% in 1992 to 30% in 2022, with substantial regional variation, while 15.2% of adult women and 5% of adult men are now living with obesity, with diabetes affecting roughly 7% of adults.

The Tanzanian opportunity is generational. The current school-age cohort is the first growing up in a country where neither hunger nor processed-food abundance fully dominates — the choices their parents make about ugali, mboga, fish, and dagaa are still intact, but the soda, biscuit, and chip industries are arriving fast. Caps Kids reaches a Tanzanian 11-year-old at exactly the moment the food environment is shifting around them, and gives them a framework — Cap, the plate, the rotation of vegetables and protein and starch — that locks in the inheritance their grandmother is already trying to pass on. The Awana network in Tanzania has the trust and the room; Caps Kids has the curriculum.

### Uganda

Uganda sits at a level of hunger the Global Hunger Index classifies as serious, and child stunting remains stubbornly high despite economic progress. Uganda ranks 87th out of 123 countries in the 2025 Global Hunger Index with a score of 20.2, placing it in the "serious" category; national stunting prevalence sits around 41%, well above the East African average.

Ugandan child nutrition is bottlenecked by complementary feeding — what children eat between the ages of one and two, and the dietary monotony that follows. By the time a Ugandan child reaches the 5th-grade-floor that Caps Kids targets, the developmental window for stunting has closed, but a different window has opened: the child can now be the one who learns, who teaches younger siblings, who pushes back on the pattern. This is the wedge for Caps Kids. The plate library, the rotation of legumes and greens and animal-source foods, the cooking-mode walkthroughs — these turn a 10-year-old into a household nutrition node. In families where the mother is overworked or absent, the older child carrying real food competence is not an edge case. It's a typical case.

### Rwanda

Rwanda has made measurable progress on stunting but remains one of the most affected countries in the world, with rural and poor districts still in crisis. Rwanda's national stunting prevalence is among the highest globally, with substantial reduction over two decades but persistent rural concentration; in Ngoma District, 33.7% of children 6–59 months were stunted, with poor diet, lack of vegetables, and inadequate sanitation as drivers.

The Ngoma findings are diagnostic for the whole country: kids weren't eating vegetables, weren't eating fish, weren't eating eggs. Caps Kids can't fix poverty, but it can fix the much narrower gap between "this food exists in your village" and "you and your child know what to do with it." A Rwandan plate library entry — built around umutsima, beans, dodo greens, and small fish — is exactly the sort of curriculum module that translates a UNICEF policy goal into a single 11-year-old's actual evening. Run it offline on the phone the older brother already has, share it through the Awana club already meeting in the church basement, and you have the cheapest delivery mechanism for nutrition education ever built in that country.

### Burundi

Burundi has, by some measures, the highest child stunting prevalence on the continent and one of the highest in the world. Burundi's stunting prevalence has been measured at roughly 47% — the kind of number that is no longer "high" but structural, embedded in the food system itself.

In Burundi, Caps Kids is not principally a behavior-change tool — at this level of structural deprivation, behavior is downstream of access. But it is a literacy tool, and that matters in two specific ways. First, it gives Burundian families and teachers a shared vocabulary for what a complete meal looks like, even when the components are unaffordable on a given day — and that vocabulary becomes actionable on the day they are affordable. Second, it gives Awana-trained Burundian teachers a curriculum-grade resource that doesn't require electricity or connectivity, which means it actually deploys. The plate library is the non-negotiable here. Cap is the friendly tutor. The on-device build is what makes it real.

### Malawi

Malawi sits among the East African countries with the highest stunting burden and ranks consistently in the upper tier of African food-insecurity indices. Malawian stunting was measured at 47.1% in earlier DHS rounds — among the highest in East Africa — alongside acute food insecurity tied to climate shocks and a heavily maize-dependent diet.

The Malawi pattern — high stunting, monotonous nsima-heavy diets, micronutrient gaps — is precisely what Caps Kids' plate library was designed to counteract at the kid level. Teaching a Malawian 5th-grader to think of nsima as the starch base and then deliberately rotate beans, ndiwo greens, groundnuts, and fish around it is not a Western imposition; it's a framework for what Malawian grandmothers already know but is being lost to time pressure and processed convenience. Awana's existing relationships with Malawian teachers create a delivery channel that does not require building anything new — only adding the food literacy module to a curriculum already in motion.

### Zambia

Zambia is one of the African countries hit hardest by the 2024 El Niño drought, layered onto an existing high stunting burden. Zambia is classified by UNICEF/WHO/World Bank as having very-high stunting prevalence, and the El Niño shock caused widespread crop failures and power shortages across Southern Africa, with Zambia and Zimbabwe particularly affected.

The Zambian case shows where Caps Kids' offline architecture earns its keep. When power goes out for hours daily and connectivity is intermittent, a cloud-dependent app is dead weight. A device-resident Gemma model that can walk a Zambian 12-year-old through what to do with the maize and dried fish and rape greens that *are* available — that's a tool that holds together when infrastructure doesn't. Zambia is also the country where Awana's missions network and the youth pastor model overlap — Caps Kids extends what an under-resourced youth leader can offer kids without adding to that leader's plate.

### Mozambique

Mozambique carries one of the highest stunting rates in Southern Africa, and it is one of the countries most exposed to climate shocks and cyclones along its coast. Stunting prevalence in Mozambique has been measured at 42.8%, the highest among the 13 East and Southern African countries in the WHO priority list.

Mozambique is a place where Caps Kids becomes a tool for cultural preservation as much as nutrition. The country has a rich Portuguese-Swahili-Bantu food tradition built around xima, matapa with cassava leaves, dried fish, peanuts, and coastal seafood — a tradition with extraordinary nutritional density when intact and a tradition under pressure from imported processed foods in coastal cities. A Mozambican plate library entry teaches the 5th-grader why the food their grandmother grew up with is, in fact, better than the chips and soft drinks creeping in from the city. Cap becomes a translator across that generational gap.

### Ethiopia

Ethiopia carries the highest stunting burden in East Africa by absolute number, with longstanding regional variation and recurring conflict-driven food crises. Ethiopian stunting has been measured at roughly 53.9%, among the highest in Africa, and Ethiopia is one of the sub-Saharan African countries identified as having very high prevalence of both stunting and wasting in the latest UNICEF/WHO/World Bank estimates.

Ethiopia is a complex case for Caps Kids. The country's food culture is one of the richest and most distinctive on the continent — injera, wat, shiro, the fasting-day vegetable tradition — and that culture is, when intact, a model of vegetable-forward, legume-rich eating. The Caps Kids opening is not to teach Ethiopian families to eat differently; it is to give Ethiopian children a structured way to *understand* what they're already eating, to value it, and to recognize when it is being eroded. In displaced or conflict-affected populations, Caps Kids' offline-capable design becomes the determining feature: the curriculum still works in a refugee camp where there is no signal.

### Ghana

Ghana sits in the moderate range for West African stunting but has well-documented gaps in complementary feeding practices for children 6–23 months, and an emerging adolescent obesity layer in urban areas. Only about half of Ghanaian children 6–23 months meet minimum meal frequency standards, alongside elevated stunting and underweight, and Ghana is part of the rapid urban dietary transition reshaping West African child nutrition.

Caps Kids in Ghana — and Frank, the local Awana leader quoted on AWANA's own pages, would recognize this — is the bridge between the village food tradition and the Accra schoolyard. Banku, fufu, light soup, kelewele, kontomire — these are foundational nutritional architectures. The Caps Kids plate library helps a Ghanaian kid see the structure inside those dishes (starch, vegetable, protein, fat), which is what makes them resilient against the chip-and-soda alternative being marketed to them. The 5th-grade age floor is the right entry point: that's the age Ghanaian kids start having pocket money and making their own snack choices.

### Liberia

Liberia carries one of the highest under-five mortality rates in West Africa, and child malnutrition is a significant contributor. Liberia has child mortality rates around 49%, the highest in West Africa, with growth failures like stunting and underweight significantly more prevalent in countries with the highest mortality; complementary feeding gaps are well-documented, with low minimum-meal-frequency rates among children 6–23 months.

Liberia is meaningful symbolically for Awana — it is where Awana first arrived in Africa under John Gaye in 1990. Caps Kids' Liberian use case sits at the older end of the age-band: an 11- or 12-year-old, often already managing meals for younger siblings in households shaped by Liberia's long recovery from war and Ebola. Giving that child a structured, low-friction tool — Cap, the plate, the cooking mode that walks them through a real meal — is direct relief, not abstract education. The on-device design matters here too: Liberian connectivity remains uneven, and a curriculum that fails when the data runs out is a curriculum that fails Liberian kids.

### Nigeria

Nigeria has both the largest absolute number of stunted children in West Africa and an enormous internal range, with Northern Nigeria approaching crisis levels. Nigerian stunting has been measured at 36.8% in West African meta-analysis, with underweight at 28.7%, while urban Nigerian children face an emerging obesity transition driven by the same global ultra-processed food forces.

Nigeria is the most populous country on the continent and the one where Caps Kids' scale potential is largest. Awana's existing Nigerian footprint creates a delivery channel that doesn't require building distribution from scratch. The Northern Nigeria use case is closer to the Burundi/Ethiopia pattern — literacy, vocabulary, and offline-capable delivery in places where structural deprivation is the limiting factor. The Lagos/Abuja use case is closer to the Kenya/Ghana pattern — countering processed-food marketing in a child population with rising obesity rates. The plate library handles both, because the underlying skill — read the plate, build the plate — is the same.

### Sierra Leone

Sierra Leone has West Africa's third-highest stunting rate and one of the highest under-five mortality rates globally. Sierra Leonean stunting has been measured at 37.9%, among the highest in West Africa; child mortality remains around 46%, with growth failures and maternal factors as primary drivers.

Sierra Leone is a country where Caps Kids functions as an extension of the same teacher-relationship model that Awana already runs. The country's recovery from Ebola, civil war, and recurring economic shocks has produced a generation of children who are deeply resilient and chronically under-fed. A 5th-grader learning, through Cap, how to make a complete meal from cassava leaves, dried fish, palm oil, and rice — and then teaching that to a younger sibling — is a small but real intervention against intergenerational nutrition loss. The curriculum costs nothing additional to deploy on top of an Awana club already meeting weekly.

### South Africa

South Africa is the cleanest example on the continent of a true double burden — significant stunting alongside the highest childhood overweight rate in East and Southern Africa. South African stunting has been measured at roughly 22% alongside childhood overweight prevalence of 13.5%, the highest in the East and Southern African region; the country shows the pattern where overweight concentrates in higher-income households while undernutrition concentrates in poor ones.

South Africa is where Caps Kids' single-platform design solves a problem that single-issue programs cannot. A nutrition program that only addresses stunting misses the urban Johannesburg child whose family can afford McDonald's; a program that only addresses obesity misses the rural Limpopo child living on white maize porridge. Caps Kids' plate library, by being structurally about *what makes a complete plate* rather than *how to eat less* or *how to eat more*, applies the same skill to both halves of the population. Combine that with Awana's relational footprint in South African townships and rural areas, and you have a tool that meets the country where it actually is — not where simplifying assumptions place it.

## EUROPE — The Awana Europe/North Asia Footprint

### United Kingdom

The UK has one of the highest childhood obesity rates in the OECD and one of the most pronounced obesity-deprivation gradients in Europe. 22.2% of UK Year 6 children (ages 10–11) are living with obesity, with prevalence reaching 29.3% in the most deprived areas versus 13.5% in the least deprived; approximately 1 in 4 UK households with children have experienced food insecurity, and the poorest fifth of households would need to spend roughly 50% of their disposable income to afford a recommended healthy diet.

The UK case is the case Christopher already understands instinctively from East Tennessee — same shape, different accent. A British 10-year-old in a deprived ward in Newcastle or Glasgow lives in the same food environment as a kid in Knoxville: fast food on every corner, processed food cheaper than vegetables, a parent or carer working hours that make scratch cooking aspirational. Caps Kids' response in the UK is the same as its response in East Tennessee — Cap teaches the kid to cook, the plate library shows what a complete meal actually looks like, the 5th-grade age floor catches them right before the obesity gap doubles between primary and Year 6. Awana UK has the church-basement infrastructure. Caps Kids fits inside it without modification.

### Germany

Germany shows a moderate but stubbornly stable pattern of childhood overweight, with sharp inequalities between regions and immigrant communities. In the Bremen federal state, where COSI data is collected, 27% of 7–9 year olds are overweight (including obese) and 12% are living with obesity, with no significant decline in recent rounds.

Germany was AWANA's first European country, and German child nutrition is a study in how affluence does not automatically protect against bad food. Caps Kids' German use case is closest to the UK's — same processed-food saturation, same time-pressed parents, same kid-aimed marketing — with the additional layer of substantial migrant communities whose food traditions are powerful but unevenly transmitted. The plate library's multi-cuisine architecture is well-suited to a country where a Berlin classroom may include Turkish, Syrian, Russian, and German kids; teaching all of them the same underlying plate-construction skill across their respective cuisines is a feature, not a workaround.

### Spain

Spain has one of the highest childhood overweight rates in Europe, despite the broader Mediterranean diet's reputation. Spain's COSI Round 6 data shows 34% of 7-year-olds are overweight (including obese) and 15% are living with obesity; the country has, like Italy and Greece, seen erosion of the traditional Mediterranean pattern in favor of processed foods.

Spain is the case that surprises North Americans. The Mediterranean diet exists, but it is no longer what most Spanish children eat. Ultra-processed foods have moved aggressively into Spanish kid culture, snack culture, and school culture. Caps Kids' Spanish use case is essentially restoration: Cap teaching a Sevilla 10-year-old to make a real tortilla española with eggs and potatoes and onions, to recognize the difference between gazpacho from scratch and the supermarket carton, to assemble a plate that looks like what their abuela ate in the 1970s. The plate library is the carrier. The cooking-mode walkthrough is the lever.

### Italy

Italy consistently shows among the highest childhood obesity rates in Europe — particularly striking given the country's culinary reputation. Italy's COSI Round 6 data places 37% of 8-year-olds as overweight (including obese) and 17% as living with obesity, with prevalence comparable to Greece and Cyprus.

The Italian case is the cleanest demonstration that a country's adult food culture does not automatically transmit to its children. Italian 8-year-olds are not eating like Italian grandparents. They are eating brioche, snack cakes, and after-school chips. Caps Kids in Italy works essentially the same way it works in Spain: as a structured way to put real food back in front of the child, with Cap as the mediator. The cuisine — pasta with vegetables, simple meat preparations, beans, olive oil — is one of the easiest to teach in a plate-library format because the structure is so legible. Italy is also a country where Awana's footprint, while smaller, sits inside parishes and Christian school networks that are open to nutrition curriculum.

### Greece

Greece has one of the highest childhood obesity rates in Europe and one of the steepest gender gaps. Greece's COSI Round 6 data shows 37% of 7-year-olds living with overweight (including obese) and 18% with obesity, with boys substantially more affected than girls.

The Greek paradox is the same as the Italian one: a country with a globally celebrated traditional diet whose children are no longer eating it. Greek children are eating processed snacks at high frequency, and the historic protective effect of the Mediterranean pattern has thinned. Caps Kids in Greece — Cap teaching a 9-year-old how to make a real horiatiki, a real fasolada, a real pastitsio from raw materials — is a direct restoration play. The plate library entry for a Greek meal is one of the most elegant because the underlying architecture (vegetables, legumes, fish, olive oil, bread) is so coherent. Awana Greece can deliver this through existing Sunday school and youth-group rhythms.

### Poland

Poland is in the upper tier of European childhood overweight, with a sharp post-COVID rise. Poland's COSI Round 6 data shows 33% of 8-year-olds living with overweight (including obese) and 15% with obesity, with significant gender disparities favoring boys.

Poland is in transition — a country that within living memory had a different food economy and is now several decades deep into Western-style ultra-processed food penetration. Polish kids' diets show that arc clearly, and Polish parents — many of whom remember different — are receptive to interventions that point back to whole-food cooking. Caps Kids in Poland fits the cultural register exactly: Cap walks an 11-year-old in Kraków through making real bigos, real pierogi, real żurek, and the kid leaves the session with a usable skill. The Awana footprint in Poland is one of the older European ones and provides natural delivery.

### Romania

Romania is one of the European countries showing one of the largest gender gaps in childhood overweight, alongside post-COVID increases and pronounced rural poverty. Romania's COSI Round 6 data places 28% of 7-year-olds as overweight (including obese), with one of the highest boy-girl gaps in Europe at +8.6 percentage points, and rural-urban disparities remain significant.

Romania is the European country where the deprivation-obesity pattern most resembles parts of the United States: rural communities with limited fresh food access, processed foods cheap and dominant, and significant child food insecurity in Roma and rural populations. Caps Kids in Romania works in two registers — counter-marketing the chip-and-cola pattern in cities, and serving as a basic food literacy tool in poorer rural communities where families are technically food-secure but nutritionally narrow. The on-device build is relevant here too: Romanian rural connectivity is not a given.

### Bulgaria

Bulgaria has shown statistically significant increases in childhood overweight in girls since the previous COSI round, against a backdrop of one of Europe's lower-income economies. Bulgaria's COSI Round 6 data shows 33% of 7-year-olds with overweight (including obese) and 15% with obesity, with the increase in girls' overweight (+4.1 percentage points) among the largest in Europe.

Bulgaria's curve is moving in the wrong direction at exactly the age band Caps Kids targets. The cultural soil — banitsa, lyutenitsa, shopska salad, beans, yogurt — is the kind of cuisine that, when taught structurally, gives kids a defensible position against the processed-food drift. Caps Kids' plate library handled with care for Bulgarian dishes gives Awana Bulgaria a youth-group resource that ties directly to family heritage rather than imposing a foreign framework.

### Hungary

Hungary has a moderate but persistent childhood overweight pattern with limited recent improvement. Hungary's COSI Round 6 data shows 27% of 7-year-olds with overweight (including obese) and 13% with obesity, with the gap between deprived and non-deprived populations widening over the last decade.

The Hungarian use case for Caps Kids is similar to Romania's — a country whose traditional cuisine (gulyás, paprikás, töltött káposzta, lecsó) is structurally sound but increasingly displaced by industrial snack foods in kids' diets. Caps Kids restores the line between what the grandmother knows and what the grandchild can do, with Cap as the technological bridge.

### Albania

Albania shows a high childhood overweight rate with one of Europe's largest boy-girl gaps. Albania's COSI Round 6 data shows 31% of 8-year-olds with overweight (including obese), with a +5.2 percentage-point gap favoring boys.

Albania is a smaller market but a strategically important one because its Awana footprint is well-established and its cuisine — fërgesë, byrek, qofte, the rich Mediterranean-Balkan vegetable tradition — is one of the easier ones for Caps Kids' plate library to teach. Albania also represents the broader Balkans-and-southeastern-Europe band where Awana's regional framework operates and where childhood obesity has been rising while traditional food culture is being eroded.

### Ukraine

Ukraine's childhood nutrition picture is shaped by an active war — disrupted food production, displaced populations, and elevated risk for both undernutrition in war-affected regions and ultra-processed reliance in displacement contexts. Ukraine's COSI Round 6 data, gathered during wartime, shows 22% of 7-year-olds with overweight (including obese) and 8% with obesity; 8.9 million Ukrainians faced food insecurity at the height of the disruption, declining to 7.3 million by 2024 but still very high, with substantial agricultural infrastructure damage.

Ukraine is the country in the European footprint where Caps Kids' offline-capable design has war-zone relevance. Families displaced internally, families in basements during air raids, families in regions where the power grid is intermittently destroyed — these are populations where a cloud-dependent app is useless and a device-resident Gemma model that can teach a 12-year-old how to put together a real meal from whatever is in the cupboard is genuinely useful. The Awana network in Ukraine has been remarkably resilient through the war. Caps Kids fits inside the existing children's ministry rhythm without requiring infrastructure that wartime conditions strip away.

## NORTH AND CENTRAL ASIA

### Russia

Russia carries among Europe's highest adult obesity rates and has limited public child nutrition data, but the broader food environment shows the pattern that produces it. Russian adult obesity sits at 28.7% for women and 20.3% for men, with diabetes affecting roughly 8% of adults; Russia is currently classified in the low-hunger category in the 2025 Global Hunger Index.

The Russian case is one where Caps Kids meets a country with a robust food culture (borshch, kasha, pelmeni, pirozhki, the whole cabbage-and-root-vegetable winter tradition) that has been increasingly displaced in children's everyday eating by industrial snack foods and Western fast-food chains. Cap teaching a Russian 11-year-old to make real shchi or real plov is a direct restoration of cultural and nutritional inheritance. Awana's Russian operations have always been delicate; the curriculum's ability to deploy in church or home settings without state involvement matters.

### Mongolia

Mongolia is one of the cleanest North Asian examples of the double burden, with dramatically rising adult obesity and persistent under-five overweight alongside historical stunting. Mongolian under-five overweight prevalence sits at 10.5%, with adult obesity at 26.5% in women and 20.9% in men, and diabetes at 13–14% of adults.

Mongolia's food environment is undergoing one of the fastest shifts on the continent — a pastoral, meat-and-dairy-heavy traditional diet now layered with imported processed foods in Ulaanbaatar and an emerging child obesity wave. Caps Kids in Mongolia does the same restoration work as in Russia, with the additional advantage that traditional Mongolian foods (buuz, khuushuur, suutei tsai, dairy-based dishes) are protein-dense, whole-food, and structurally well-suited to plate-library treatment. The on-device build is essential in a country where rural and ger-district connectivity is uneven.

### Kazakhstan

Kazakhstan is the most economically advanced Central Asian country and shows the early stages of the double burden. Kazakhstan (Almaty) COSI Round 6 data shows 19% of 8-year-olds with overweight (including obese) and 7% with obesity, on the lower end of the European Region but trending upward; the broader Central Asia and Caucasus region has seen obesity rates among children rise more than 80% since 2000 alongside persistent stunting.

Kazakhstan is the platform country for Central Asia — both because of its size and because its government has shown openness to school nutrition policy work. Caps Kids in Kazakhstan addresses the rising obesity curve in Almaty and Astana while remaining relevant for rural Kazakh families whose food economy still resembles Mongolia's. Kazakh cuisine (beshbarmak, plov, manti, kuyrdak) plate-library entries restore the traditional pattern. Awana's Central Asian footprint, while constrained, has Kazakhstan as one of its more accessible operating environments.

### Kyrgyzstan

Kyrgyzstan shows the Central Asian triple burden in one of its sharpest forms, with stunting, micronutrient deficiency, and rising child overweight all present. In Kyrgyzstan, 47% of children under five have iron deficiency, 20.9% are anemic, and stunting reaches 23% in some oblasts; meanwhile, around one in ten children under five is now overweight, and 75% of children 6–23 months consumed unhealthy food in the last day per the 2023 MICS.

Kyrgyzstan is the country where Caps Kids has perhaps its strongest single-product use case in Central Asia — every dimension of the triple burden maps onto something the curriculum directly addresses. The plate library teaches structural meal-building (countering both monotonous stunting-driving diets and processed-food obesity-driving ones); the cooking mode teaches scratch cooking with whole foods (counters processed reliance); the cuisine itself (lagman, manty, plov, fresh dairy, mountain herbs) is robust when intact. The on-device build is essential — Kyrgyz mountain-village connectivity is what it is.

### Tajikistan

Tajikistan is the poorest Central Asian country and the most food-insecure, with significant child stunting and household-level food insecurity. Roughly 30% of Tajiks face difficulty feeding themselves; 80% of household expenditure goes to food; about 8% of children are underweight and 15% are stunted, though estimates vary.

Tajikistan's case is closer to a sub-Saharan African pattern than to a European one. Caps Kids in Tajikistan operates as a literacy and skills tool against a backdrop of structural deprivation — teaching the older child to make the most of available ingredients (osh, kurutob, mantu, bread, vegetables when available, dried fruit) and to teach younger siblings. The curriculum cannot fix Tajik poverty, but it can ensure that a Tajik 11-year-old in a remote Pamir village has more food competence than her mother had at the same age, on the same household budget. The offline build makes that delivery actually possible.

### Uzbekistan

Uzbekistan has made significant progress on stunting reduction but carries elevated adult obesity and a triple-burden pattern in younger children. Uzbekistan's under-five stunting sits at 10.8%, on track for global targets; adult obesity is 21.8% in women and 16.1% in men, both above regional averages, with diabetes around 13% of adults; Uzbekistan ranks in the medium-malnutrition tier per UNICEF's child food poverty analysis.

Uzbekistan is the Central Asian country where Caps Kids' "double-direction" architecture matters most. The country has largely solved the worst undernutrition while moving briskly into the obesity transition; a child today is more likely to face the chip-and-soda problem than the not-enough-rice problem. The Uzbek cuisine — plov, shurpa, samsa, lagman, fresh non-bread, tomato-cucumber salads — is structurally healthy when prepared traditionally. Caps Kids' plate-library treatment of Uzbek cooking reinforces the existing food culture against the marketing forces eroding it.

## Synthesis — Why Caps Kids Fits This Map

The country-by-country detail above reduces, when stepped back from, to a small number of structural facts that make Caps Kids unusually well-suited to this footprint.

**The same skill addresses both ends of the spectrum.** Stunting in Burundi and obesity in Cyprus look like opposite problems, but the underlying intervention — teaching a child to read, build, and prepare a complete plate — is the same skill in both contexts. Most nutrition programs are direction-specific; Caps Kids is structurally direction-agnostic, which means it deploys to the African Awana club and the British Awana club without rewriting the curriculum.

**The on-device architecture is a deployment requirement, not a feature.** A cloud-dependent app fails in rural Zimbabwe, in wartime Ukraine, in a Tajik mountain village, and in a Liberian neighborhood with intermittent power. Caps Kids' device-resident Gemma 4 E4B build is the version that actually reaches the children Awana already reaches. This is the single most important technical decision in the product.

**The plate library is culturally translatable, not culturally imposed.** The mirepoix plate is the visual reference; the Zimbabwean sadza-and-greens plate, the Ghanaian banku-and-soup plate, the Kazakh beshbarmak plate, the Italian pasta-with-vegetables plate are all the same teaching object in different cuisines. Caps Kids does not ask any country to eat like America. It asks each country to eat like itself, taught more deliberately to its children.

**The 5th-grade age floor is the right intervention point in every region.** In Africa, the developmental window for stunting has closed by 5th grade, but the older-sibling-as-household-nutrition-node window has opened. In Europe and North Asia, the obesity gap between primary and middle school is exactly where the 5th-grade-and-up curriculum lands. The age choice is not arbitrary; it is the moment at which the child becomes capable of acting on what they learn.

**The Awana relational footprint is the distribution channel that does not exist anywhere else.** No other organization has trusted, weekly, classroom-and-church-basement contact with school-age children in 16 African countries, 27 European-and-North-Asian countries, and 130-plus countries total. Caps Kids does not need to build distribution. It needs to build a curriculum that fits inside the distribution that already exists. That is the whole point of the partnership.

The work ahead — the cloud prototype, the first plate batch, the AWANA prospectus that follows — is what turns this from a thesis on paper into a tool actually in the hands of the kid in Mt Darwin, the kid in Kraków, the kid in Bishkek. That kid is the same kid as the one in East Tennessee whose mother works two jobs. The mission is the same. The map is just larger.
