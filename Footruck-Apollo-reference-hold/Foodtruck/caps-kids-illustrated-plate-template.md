# Captain Culinary Kids — Illustrated Plate Library
## Style Template & Commissioning Brief

> **For:** the illustrator commissioned to build the Caps Kids curriculum plate library
> **From:** Christopher Moore, Founder, Blue Collar Apps Co.
> **Reference plate:** the mirepoix plate dated April 2026 (attached)
> **Purpose:** define the visual template and commissioning standard so every future plate in the Caps Kids library reads as one coherent voice

---

## What we're building

Captain Culinary Kids is a childhood food literacy platform for American children fifth grade and up. The platform teaches cooking through a still-image-plus-voice pedagogy — no fast-cut chef videos, no rushing. The kid sets the tempo. Cap (the AI instructor) narrates over magazine-grade illustrated plates that the kid can study at their own pace.

The plate library is the curriculum's visual backbone. Each plate teaches one technique, one ingredient, or one foundational preparation through hand-illustrated, watercolor-style botanical-instructional artwork. The mirepoix plate is the template. Every future plate must share its voice.

This document specifies that voice in enough detail that any plate commissioned against it will land in the same family.

## §1 — The visual register

### Drawing technique

Watercolor-style botanical-instructional illustration. The reference is the long tradition of hand-illustrated culinary instruction — Cook's Illustrated step photography reimagined as illustration, the Larousse Gastronomique plates, classical herbal manuscripts, the visual feel of an heirloom kitchen reference book. Soft edges, warm color, painterly fills with light pencil-line definition, subtle shadow grounding each subject without harsh drop shadow.

The objects must read as objects, not as decoration. A 5th grader looking at a single illustration of a small-diced onion should be able to *recognize* a small-diced onion when they see one in their own kitchen. Pedagogical clarity is the highest priority. Beauty serves clarity. When in tension, clarity wins.

### Palette

- **Background:** soft cream (#F5EFE0 to #FAF4E5 range), slightly warmer than pure cream
- **Primary illustration colors:** natural, earthy, true to the actual color of the food — not stylized, not punched, not muted into pastels. A carrot is carrot-orange. Celery is celery-green. An onion's papery skin is its actual papery brown.
- **Background doodle accents:** a small palette of soft secondary colors used at low opacity (10–20%) — dusty teal, soft coral, butter yellow, sage. Used only at the edges, never over the teaching content.
- **Arrow accent (progression markers):** soft terracotta or muted coral. Not bright orange. Not red. The arrows must read as helpful, not urgent.

### Typography

- **Subject and label captions** (e.g., *Onion, Trimmed, Slices, Batons, Small Dice*): italic display serif, warm dark color, kept small and elegant. Not bold. Not loud. The captions support the illustration — they don't lead it.
- **Section title / featured technique** (e.g., *MIREPOIX*): large display serif in *alternating soft brand colors* across the letters. Playful but disciplined. The colors come from the brand palette: the teal, coral, butter yellow, sage. Each letter a different one. *This is the one place where playfulness reads loudest.* It's deliberate — it de-fangs intimidating culinary vocabulary for the kid.
- **Subtitle below the technique title** (e.g., *From Vegetable to Small Dice*): script font in soft teal, lower weight, classical feel.
- **Sidebar definition text** (e.g., *A classic aromatic foundation for soups, sauces…*): same italic display serif as captions, slightly larger, in a warm dark tone.

### Composition rules

- **Top:** Captain Culinary Kids logo lockup, centered. Always present.
- **Body:** the teaching grid — typically a row-by-row progression of states or a category-by-category layout, depending on what's being taught. Plenty of breathing room between subjects. No background fills inside the grid.
- **Bottom:** the *finished example* image (the realized form of what the plate teaches — e.g., a bowl of mirepoix, a finished hard-boiled egg cross-section, a jar of clarified butter), set alongside a brief sidebar definition.
- **Lower band:** the technique title in alternating-color display type, with the script subtitle below it.
- **Background:** soft doodles at the corners only — kitchen tools, stars, dotted lines, pastel color blobs at low opacity. *Never inside the teaching grid.*

### What the plate is NOT

To be explicit:

- Not Pixar. Not 3D-rendered. Not glossy-cartoon.
- Not photographs.
- Not Saveur-style moody editorial. Not Bon Appétit cinematic.
- Not Disney/Marvel character-driven.
- Not infographic-style with charts, percentages, or data viz.
- Not loud, busy, or visually competitive. The page must breathe.
- Not condescending. The kid is treated as a real learner. The plate would not look out of place hung in a serious adult kitchen — that is the bar.

## §2 — The Caps Kids logo lockup

The captain's hat is the Caps Kids brand mark. It's a small naval captain's cap with a star on the front, rendered in soft watercolor matching the plate. It appears at the top of every plate.

Below the hat, the wordmark **CAPTAIN CULINARY KIDS** appears. *Note: on the mirepoix reference, the lockup stacks as CAPTAIN (large) / CULINARY KIDS (small) — this works but feels slightly hierarchy-confused. For all future plates, render it as a clean three-line stack: CAPTAIN / CULINARY / KIDS, each line equal weight, classical display serif, dark warm color, centered.* Or alternatively as two lines: CAPTAIN CULINARY (one line) / KIDS (centered below). Lock the chosen version on the next plate and use it identically forever after.

Do not repeat the logo at the bottom of the plate. The mirepoix reference has a small lower lockup that's visually redundant. Cut it from future plates. Let the technique title (e.g., *MIREPOIX*) serve as the closer.

## §3 — The plate types

The library will eventually cover dozens of techniques and ingredients. They fall into three structural plate types, each with its own grid logic. Specify the plate type when commissioning.

### Plate Type A — Progression (the mirepoix model)

A subject (or set of related subjects) shown across stages from raw to finished. Read left-to-right.

- **Header row:** column labels at the top describing each stage (e.g., *Whole · Trimmed & Prepped · Slices/Planks · Batons/Strips · Small Dice*).
- **Body rows:** one row per subject; each row shows the same subject at each stage, with stage captions below each illustration. Soft arrows (terracotta) between stages indicating progression.
- **Bottom band:** the finished combined preparation in a bowl, plus a sidebar definition.

**Future Type A plates might include:**
- *Knife Cuts* — a single subject (potato, say) shown across all classical cuts: brunoise / small dice / medium dice / large dice / batonnet / julienne / fine julienne / chiffonade
- *Aromatics* — onion, garlic, shallot, leek, scallion, each shown raw → trimmed → sliced → minced
- *Eggs* — egg shown across preparations: in shell / cracked / whisked / soft scrambled / hard boiled / poached
- *Stocks* — bones / vegetables / herbs / pot / strained finished stock

### Plate Type B — Anatomy (the cross-section model)

A single technique or preparation shown in detail with labeled parts and stages. Used for techniques where the *internal structure* is what's being taught.

- **Hero illustration:** a large, central, beautifully rendered cross-section or detail view of the finished thing.
- **Annotation labels:** small italic captions with thin connecting lines pointing to specific parts (e.g., on a hard-boiled egg cross-section: *firm white · just-set yolk · no green ring · clean shell separation*).
- **Side panel:** a vertical column showing the steps that produced the result, each step a small illustration with a one-line caption.

**Future Type B plates might include:**
- *Hard-Boiled Egg* — cross-sections of perfectly cooked vs. overcooked, with timing chart
- *Roasted Chicken* — a finished bird with parts labeled, plus side panel showing prep stages
- *Bread Loaf* — cross-section of a finished loaf with crumb structure labeled, plus side stages

### Plate Type C — Process (the recipe model)

A short sequence of steps shown as a numbered visual recipe, used for techniques that are simple enough to fit on one plate but require ordered execution.

- **Header:** the technique name and yield (e.g., *Clarified Butter — Yields 1 cup*).
- **Body:** four to eight numbered illustration panels in sequence, each with a one-line italic caption beneath.
- **Bottom band:** the finished product (e.g., a jar of golden clarified butter) with a sidebar definition explaining what it's used for.

**Future Type C plates might include:**
- *Clarified Butter* — six panels: butter in pan / melting / foaming / skim foam / clear gold / pour through cheesecloth
- *Vinaigrette* — five panels: shallot in vinegar / mustard added / oil drizzled / whisked emulsion / dressed greens
- *Caramelizing Onions* — six panels showing the slow color shift over 45 minutes
- *Béchamel* — roux / hot milk / whisk / thickening / finished sauce

## §4 — The first commission batch

For the launch curriculum (10–12 year-old band, foundational techniques), the first commissioning batch should be twelve plates:

1. **Mirepoix** — *already complete* (Type A, the reference plate)
2. **Knife Cuts** — Type A — single potato across the full classical-cut family
3. **Aromatics** — Type A — onion, garlic, shallot, leek, scallion
4. **Eggs** — Type A — six preparations from one egg
5. **Hard-Boiled Egg** — Type B — perfect-vs-overcooked cross-sections, timing chart
6. **Clarified Butter** — Type C — six-panel process
7. **Vinaigrette** — Type C — five-panel process
8. **Caramelizing Onions** — Type C — six-panel slow color shift
9. **Roux** — Type C — white roux to brown roux color stages
10. **Stock** — Type B — bones, vegetables, herbs labeled, with side panel of straining
11. **Pasta Shapes** — Type A — six common shapes with their use cases
12. **Bread Loaf** — Type B — finished loaf cross-section, crumb anatomy, side panel of stages

These twelve cover roughly 80% of the technique foundation a 5th-grader needs to be a real cook. Commission them as a batch with a unified style guide so they land together.

## §5 — Commissioning standards (the legal and quality terms)

### Ownership

All artwork is **work-for-hire with full assignment of copyright to Blue Collar Apps Co.** The illustrator is paid a fair commissioning fee per plate; in exchange, BCA owns the resulting artwork outright with full rights to use, modify, reproduce, and license. The illustrator may include the work in their portfolio with a credit line we agree on.

### Format & deliverables

For each plate, the illustrator delivers:
- High-resolution master file (300 DPI minimum, 16×20 inches at full size)
- Layered source file (Photoshop, Procreate, or Affinity Photo native — we need access to layers for future modifications)
- Two crops: square (1:1, for app and social), and portrait (4:5, the print version)
- Transparent-background versions of any reusable element (e.g., the bowl from the mirepoix plate could be reused on other plates if isolated)

### Style consistency check

Before approving a plate, run this checklist:

- Does the cream background match the mirepoix reference within ±5% color value?
- Are the captions in the same italic display serif as the reference?
- Is the technique title rendered in alternating brand colors (teal, coral, butter yellow, sage) across the letters?
- Is the script subtitle in soft teal?
- Are the progression arrows soft terracotta (not bright orange)?
- Are background doodles confined to the corners and below 25% opacity?
- Does the Caps Kids logo lockup at the top match the standardized form?
- Is there *any* element of Pixar / 3D / cartoon / photograph / stock infographic in the rendering? If yes, reject and revise.

### Pricing guidance

Botanical-instructional illustration of this caliber commissions in the range of **$200–$600 per plate** depending on the illustrator's stature and the plate's complexity. Type C process plates with more panels may run higher than Type A or B. Budget for the twelve-plate launch batch in the range of $3,000–$6,000 total, with another $1,000–$2,000 reserved for revisions.

## §6 — Holding the line

Two notes for the founder, not the illustrator:

**Don't commission piecemeal.** Plate-by-plate commissioning produces inconsistent style drift even with the best illustrator. Commission the full batch of twelve as a single project, with the mirepoix as the anchor reference, and have the illustrator deliver them in waves of three or four with style-check between waves. Lock the first wave's voice before authorizing the second.

**Don't AI-generate the plates.** Current image generation models can produce *something* in this register, but they cannot produce *this register specifically* with consistent quality across twelve plates. The botanical-instructional voice, the caption typography, the precise color discipline, the painterly hand — these will drift across AI-generated plates in ways that destroy the library's coherence. *Pay the illustrator. The library is curriculum infrastructure that lasts a generation. Treat it like a generational asset.*

**Don't deviate from the mirepoix reference without explicit reason.** The reference is the standard. Future plates may need to modify the grid logic (Type B and Type C plates have different structures), but the *voice* — the colors, the typography, the doodles, the captain's hat lockup, the quiet breathing of the page — does not change. Anyone tempted to "freshen up" the look on plate seven is wrong. *The point is consistency. Twenty plates that all share one voice are worth more than twenty plates that each look great individually.*
